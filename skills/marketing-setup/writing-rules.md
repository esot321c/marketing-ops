# Writing rules (shared across all brands)

Every brand's content follows these rules. They are about credibility and about not reading as
AI-generated. They are **universal**. They do not change between brands and should not be rewritten
per tenant. A brand's voice (person, tone, vocabulary, pillars) layers on top in its own `voice.md`.

This applies to all generated copy: posts, carousels, captions, profile sections, and the example
copy inside design-system previews.

> Note: this file follows its own rules. There are no em dashes, arrows, or rhetorical triads in it.

## Person (set per brand, never mixed)

Each tenant's `voice.md` fixes the person and uses only that one. Pick the person that fits the brand:

- A personal brand (a named individual): first person singular, **"I"**.
- An agency or company brand: first person plural, **"we"**.
- A product or SaaS brand: second person, **"you / your"**.

## The rules

- Plain keyboard punctuation only. No em dashes, no arrows, no non-keyboard characters.
- Do not lean on an em dash, an arrow, or a colon to make a sentence land, and do not swap one for
  another, because a colon is the same crutch as an em dash. If a sentence needs the pause, the
  structure is wrong, so rewrite it so it does not need one.
- No "colon then a rule-of-three list" construction, and no rule-of-three or rule-of-four cadence used
  as a rhetorical device. Naming specifics inside a normal sentence is good. The punchy triad is not.
- No negative parallelism and no comparisons against things nobody said. Avoid "not just X, but Y",
  "it isn't X, it's Y", and "the work is rarely X, it is Y".
- Defensible claims only. No superlatives or quantified specifics that imply data we do not have.
  Weaken when unsure, so "the most / always / every" becomes "can / often / a lot of / in our
  experience".
- Complete sentences, never fragments. Fragments read as AI marketing copy.
- Never claim the brand uses a product, tool, or service unless it has been confirmed.
- Always the Oxford comma.
- Vary sentence structure and length. Do not open every line with the same verb, and vary list
  lengths.
- Prefer specifics over abstractions. Name the tool, the metric, the customer.
- No cleft sentences or slogan-shaped lessons. State what happened, then state what changes now.
- Open with what happened, then explain why. The takeaway follows the events that earn it, never the
  reverse.
- Claims about a system's behavior end where the brand's involvement ended.
- Avoid AI-isms and filler ("is exactly my world", "lives at the intersection", "in today's
  fast-paced world", "unlock", "elevate", "seamless", "leverage", "game-changer").

## Self-check before publishing

1. Could a skeptical reader call any claim unsupported? Weaken it or add the specific.
2. Does any sentence need an em dash, an arrow, or a colon to land? Rewrite the structure so it does
   not, rather than swapping the punctuation.
3. Any fragment, triad list, or "not X, but Y"? Remove it.
4. Is there a concrete tool, metric, or decision early in the piece?
5. Does it read as a finding or a lesson, not an announcement?
